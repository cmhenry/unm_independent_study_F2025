The Three Core Principles

Grimmer & Stewart argue that all automated text analysis should follow three fundamental principles:

  1. All methods make assumptions
  2. Validation is essential
  3. Models augment, not replace, human understanding

Let's examine each in detail...

Principle 1: All Methods Make Assumptions

"All quantitative models of language are wrong‚Äîbut some are useful"

  • Bag of Words: Assumes word order doesn't matter
  • Dictionary Methods: Assume words have fixed meanings
  • Topic Models: Assume documents are mixtures of topics
  • Even BERT: Assumes patterns in training data generalize

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Point: No "assumption-free" method exists. Always ask: What assumptions am I making?

When Assumptions Fail

Example 1: "Bank"

  • "The bank collapsed" (financial)
  • "Walk along the river bank" (geographic)

Bag of words assumption fails ‚Äî context matters!

Example 2: Sarcasm

  • "Great job!" (positive?)
  • "Great job..." (negative?)

Dictionary methods fail ‚Äî tone matters!

This is why validation (Principle 2) is crucial!

Principle 2: Validation is Essential

"No substitutes for actually reading documents and qualitative analysis"

Types of Validation:

  • Face Validity: Do results make substantive sense?
  • Convergent Validity: Do different methods agree?
  • Predictive Validity: Do results predict outcomes?
  • Comparison to Gold Standard: Agreement with human coding?

Validation in Practice

Example: Topic Model of Congressional Speeches

  1. Face Validity: Do topics have coherent themes?
    • Topic 1: "healthcare, insurance, coverage" ‚úì
  2. Read Documents: Examine high-probability speeches
    • Are they actually about healthcare? ‚úì
  3. External Evidence: Do results align with known facts?
    • Healthcare topic peaks during ACA debate? ‚úì
  4. Hand-Coding Sample: Compare to expert judgments
    • Agreement rate: 85% ‚úì

‚ö†Ô∏è Never report results without validation!

Principle 3: Models Augment Humans

"Automated methods augment, rather than replace, careful thinking and close reading"

‚ùå Don't

  • Blindly trust algorithms
  • Skip reading documents
  • Ignore domain expertise
  • Let method drive theory

‚úÖ Do

  • Use methods to scale up analysis
  • Combine with close reading
  • Ground in substantive theory
  • Let questions drive methods

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  1. The "Cool Method" Trap

    Using advanced methods because they're trendy, not because they fit the question

  2. Black Box Analysis

    Not understanding what your method actually does

  3. Over-claiming

    Asserting methods reveal "truth" rather than useful patterns

  4. Ignoring Limitations

    Not discussing when/why your method might fail

Overview of Methods (G&S Taxonomy)

Method Type Examples Key Assumption
Dictionary Word counting, sentiment Words have fixed meanings
Supervised Classification, SVM Hand-coded sample generalizes
Unsupervised Topic models, clustering Latent structure exists

Case Study: Senate Press Releases

Research Question: What do senators emphasize in communications?

Method 1: Simple Word Counting

  • Count frequency of policy terms
  • ‚úì Transparent, interpretable
  • ‚úó Misses context, synonyms

Method 2: Topic Model

  • Discovers latent themes
  • ‚úì Finds unexpected patterns
  • ‚úó Topics need interpretation

G&S Recommendation: Use both! Triangulate findings.

Example Validation Strategy

Goal: Classify Congressional speeches as "bipartisan" vs "partisan"

  1. Train classifier on hand-coded sample (n=500)
  2. Check accuracy on held-out test set (85%)
  3. Examine errors: Where does it fail?
    • Short speeches, procedural language
  4. Face validity: Do high-probability cases make sense?
    • Read 20 random "highly partisan" speeches
  5. External validity: Do patterns match known facts?
    • Partisan speech increases near elections? ‚úì

Critical Questions to Always Ask

  1. What assumptions does my method make?
  2. Where might those assumptions fail?
  3. How can I validate my results?
  4. Do findings align with substantive knowledge?
  5. Am I using the simplest appropriate method?
  6. Can I replicate my analysis?
  7. What are the limitations of my approach?

Still Relevant in the LLM Era?

Absolutely Yes!

  • GPT-4, Claude, etc. still make assumptions
    • Training data biases, context windows, prompting effects
  • Validation even more critical
    • Black box nature makes errors harder to catch
  • Human understanding still essential
    • LLMs can't replace domain expertise
  • Simple methods often better
    • More interpretable, faster, cheaper, reproducible

Key Takeaways

  1. All quantitative models of language are wrong‚Äîbut some are useful

  3. Let questions drive methods, not the reverse

    Start simple, add complexity only when needed

  4. Combine computational and human analysis

    Algorithms augment, not replace, expertise
